How a Fed Interest Hike Affects Bitcoin

January 26, 2022January 17, 2022 by haruinvest

The Federal Reserve (Fed), the United States’ central bank, has been hinting towards a turn to austerity measures. How would Bitcoin and other assets be affected by the Fed tightening money supply on the market?

  • Fed tightening signals send market into chaos

Fed sends austerity and interest hike signals – “First rate hike in March likely”

As recently as the end of last year, the Fed has been the guardian of quantitative easing policies, which pumped money into the market. However, as inflation numbers have remained stubbornly high, the Fed has shifted to a hawkish stance (preferring restrictive monetary policies).

Following the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meeting last December, US Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell said that he would end tapering (the reduction of bond purchases) by March of this year and hold three rounds of interest hikes in 2022, and two rounds in 2023. At his confirmation hearing before the US Senate Financial Services Committee on January 11, Powell reaffirmed his intention to shift towards austerity measures, suggesting the possibility of raising interest rates more than expected if high inflation persists.

What stood out was his mention that the Fed could cut its assets in the second half of the year to reduce the market’s liquidity supply. The market was shocked by remarks indicating that the Fed could go beyond tapering – reducing the bonds it had regularly purchased to stimulate the economy – and adjusting the interest rate. Beyond these measures, Powell indicated that the Fed was considering ‘quantitative tightening’, in which it would sell its assets.

The market currently expects the Fed to raise interest rates a total of 4 times this year, starting in March. On January 9, the Financial Times, citing investor reports from major banks, reported that the March hike rumors are gaining traction in the industry. On the 7th, JP Morgan Chase, which had previously predicted a raise in June, adjusted the timeline to March. On the same day, English bank Barclays also gave March as the likely time in a report. Reasoning that the Fed’s Wednesday announcement had “a greater sense of urgency” than he expected, Goldman Sachs chief economist Jan Hachius also stated on the 10th that he expected the Fed would raise its interest rate four times this year.

Fed tightening signals send market into chaos

As the Fed sends more signals of upcoming austerity measures, the investment market has been plunged into chaos. This is because unlike interest rate hikes, which indirectly reduce monetary supply, ‘quantitative tightening’ has a much bigger market impact, as the central bank directly takes money out of the market.

The Fed’s announcement sent the Dow Jones Industrial average, Standard & Poor’s (S&P), and NASDAQ indices, as well as other New York stocks plunging. Commodities, cryptocurrencies and real estate prices also fell in tandem. Shortly after the Fed released its December FOMC minutes on the 5th, the Dow Jones and S&P 500 indices fell by 1.07% and 1.94% over their previous day values, respectively. Then, on the 6th, stocks and currencies in major Asian countries also fell at once. Likewise, cryptocurrency prices sharply dropped, sending Bitcoin down 30% from its 2021 peak.

On the other hand, leading safe-haven assets such as US treasury yields and the dollar have risen. US treasury yields and the dollar have both been rising since last December. For example, the yield of 10-year US treasury bonds climbed from 1.51% at the end of 2021 to 1.8% on January 10. This 0.29%-point gain is greater than what is usually seen if the base rate is raised by 0.25 percentage points.

As the Fed increases the speed and intensity of its tightening measures, the US dollar is expected to appreciate further, increasing the likelihood of capital outflows in emerging markets. If emerging economies and various other countries decide to counter this by raising their interest rates, a major shock is expected to stocks, bonds, real estate, commodities, etc.

Kim Seong-Taek, head of the Korea Center for International Finance’s Global Economy Department told the Korea Economic Daily that “as the risk of global interest rate hikes emanating from the US increases, volatility in the global financial market may intensify, including stocks, bonds, foreign exchange and commodities”. Jun-Sam Ha, PB Team Leader at Shinhan Bank’s PWM Seoul Finance Center advised that “this year, it will be necessary to take a conservative investment strategy by creating a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, real estate/REITs, gold and commodities and then adjusting the weighting while watching economic changes following interest rate hikes”.

“Bitcoin closely tied to macro economy beyond the crypto market”

As the Fed accelerates monetary tightening signals, the financial markets are mired in uncertainty. In this situation, what scenarios are emerging for the cryptocurrency market?

One view holds that cryptocurrencies will also follow stocks on a downward trend as money on the market flows into safe haven assets due to the Fed’s interest rate hike. In an interview with CNBC, crypto broker Bitbank analyst Yuya Hasegawa cautioned that he expects Bitcoin to continue its downward trend until the market digests the likelihood of the Fed hiking interest rates in March.

The opposite view is that cryptocurrencies could replace gold as the new safe haven asset. In this positive scenario, crypto prices would rise. Zach Pandl, co-head of Goldman Sachs’ Global FX/EM Strategy believes that Bitcoin could top $100,000 in the next five years. In a January 4th note to clients, he wrote that “if Bitcoin’s share of the store of value market climbs to around 50%, the price of Bitcoin will rise to $100,000 over the next five years, with an annual return of 17-18%”.

Some analysts believe that the price of Bitcoin as the largest cryptocurrency will be more closely linked to the broader macroeconomic situation than to issues occurring within the cryptocurrency market such has DeFi (decentralized finance), Layer 2, NFT (non-fungible tokens) and the metaverse. Arca Chief Investment Officer Jeff Dorman noted that “the market dynamic of bitcoin has changed a lot in the last two years. It went from being largely, you know, crypto native [to] now, it really just trades as a 24/7 levered VIX [volatility index]”.

Marcus Sotiriou, an analyst at digital asset broker GlobalBlock, made a similar observation: “Plans declared by the Federal Reserve for rate hikes in 2022, as well as reducing the rate of monthly bond purchases, have contributed to Bitcoin’s sell off so far”, adding that “despite the crypto market being in oversold territory, it is widely impacted by global markets and economic conditions”.

In fact, the 100-day average correlation coefficient between Bitcoin and the S&P 500 stood at 0.44 as of January 6, the highest since Q4 2020. The closer this value comes to 1, the more closely two prices are correlated.
